Data Governance FAQ

Plain-language answers to the most common questions about what data is safe to use with AI tools at UNC. Updated in alignment with UNC ITS and the Office of Research.

⚠️ When in doubt, don't enter it. If you're unsure whether data is safe to use in an AI tool, treat it as restricted until you get confirmation from UNC ITS.
Can I enter research data into ChatGPT or other public AI tools? ▼
Generally, no — not without careful review. Public AI tools like ChatGPT may use your inputs to train future models. Research data — especially data covered by IRB protocols, grant agreements, or sponsor restrictions — should never be entered into public AI tools. Use only UNC-approved tools for any research-related data.

✗ Not safe for research data
What counts as "sensitive" data at UNC? ▼
UNC classifies data in three tiers: Public (safe to share freely), Sensitive (restricted access, includes student records, personnel data, unpublished research), and Restricted (HIPAA-covered, classified, or contractually protected). Only Public-tier data should be entered into non-approved AI tools.

⚠ Check classification before use
Is it safe to use AI tools for anonymized data? ▼
It depends on how the data was anonymized and which tool you're using. True anonymization that meets IRB standards may make data eligible for use in some approved tools — but "de-identified" and "anonymized" are not always the same thing. Consult with your IRB coordinator before proceeding.

⚠ Consult IRB coordinator first
Can I use AI to help write grant proposals? ▼
Yes — with important caveats. AI can help with structure, language, and editing. However, do not enter proprietary research methods, preliminary unpublished data, or sponsor-restricted information into any non-approved tool. Use UNC-approved tools only, and always disclose AI use per the funder's requirements.

✓ Approved with conditions
Does FERPA apply to AI tool use? ▼
Yes. FERPA protects student educational records, which means any student-identifiable data — names, grades, enrollment status, advising notes — cannot be entered into AI tools that are not covered by a UNC data use agreement. This applies even to seemingly harmless uses like summarizing meeting notes that contain student information.

✗ Student data requires approved tools only

Advising Scripts

Ready-to-use language for the most common AI questions you get from faculty, researchers, and colleagues. These aren't corporate-speak — they're honest, clear, and grounded in UNC policy.

"Can I use ChatGPT for my research?" ▼
Say this: "It depends on what you're using it for and what data you'd be entering. For brainstorming, structuring ideas, or working with public information — that's generally fine on the consumer version. But for anything involving your actual research data, IRB-covered subjects, grant-restricted materials, or unpublished findings, you'll want to use Microsoft Copilot through your UNC account instead. Want me to walk you through that?"
"What AI tools does UNC actually support?" ▼
Say this: "Right now, the clearest institutional support is for Microsoft Copilot — it's included in our M365 licenses and has a data use agreement that protects UNC information. Grammarly Enterprise is also covered if you use your UNC account. For other tools like Claude or Gemini, ITS hasn't completed a full review yet, so we generally recommend using public data only until that changes. I can point you to UNC AI NOW for the current tool status page."
"Is it okay to use AI to write my grant proposal?" ▼
Say this: "Yes, with a few things to keep in mind. AI is great for helping with structure, language, and editing — that part's fine. Just make sure you're not entering unpublished preliminary data, proprietary methods, or anything your funder restricts. And check the funder's requirements on AI disclosure — some are starting to require it. Use Copilot through your UNC account if you need to work with any sensitive content."
"I've been using [non-approved tool] for months. Is that a problem?" ▼
Say this: "It depends on what data you've been entering. If it was all publicly available information or your own original writing with no restricted data, you're probably fine — but going forward, it's worth moving to an approved tool to be on the safe side. If you think you may have entered sensitive data, I'd recommend checking in with UNC ITS — not to get in trouble, but to assess whether any follow-up steps are needed. They're not going to penalize you for asking."
"My department head says to use AI but I don't know which one is safe." ▼
Say this: "That's a really common situation right now and you're right to want clarity. The short answer: Microsoft Copilot is your safest bet — it's covered by UNC's data agreement and available through your existing M365 account. If your department head has a specific tool in mind, it's worth asking whether ITS has reviewed it. UNC AI NOW has a current comparison chart if that would help."

AI Disclosure Requirements

Requirements vary significantly by context. Use this guide to understand what's expected of you in research, publishing, and grant work.

⚠️ When in doubt, disclose. Most funders and publishers prefer transparency over omission. Failing to disclose when required is a more serious issue than disclosing unnecessarily.
Do I need to disclose AI use in grant proposals?▼
It depends on the funder. NSF, NIH, and most federal agencies now have explicit AI disclosure policies — check the specific program announcement. Many require disclosure if AI was used in writing the proposal narrative. Using AI for editing or grammar checking is generally not required to disclose, but using it to generate scientific content or summaries typically is.

⚠ Check funder requirements before submitting
What do journals require for AI disclosure in publications?▼
Most major publishers — including Elsevier, Springer, and Nature — now require authors to declare any use of AI tools in the methods or acknowledgments section. AI cannot be listed as an author. The key distinction most publishers make: AI used for language editing is generally acceptable with disclosure; AI used to generate or analyze data is more heavily scrutinized.

✓ Disclose in acknowledgments or methods section
Can I use AI to help write my IRB protocol?▼
Yes, with care. AI can assist with structure and language but should not be used to generate descriptions of research methodology or risk assessments that you haven't independently verified. The protocol must accurately reflect your actual research design — if AI introduces inaccuracies, that is your responsibility. Use only approved tools and avoid entering any participant-identifiable information.

⚠ Verify all AI-generated content before submission
Are there UNC-level policies on AI in research?▼
UNC's Office of Research is developing institution-wide guidance. At present, UNC follows federal agency requirements for sponsored research. Faculty are encouraged to consult with their department's research administrator and check the UNC Research website for the most current guidance, as this is a rapidly evolving area.

⚠ Check UNC Office of Research for current guidance

AI and Academic Integrity

Setting clear expectations in your course is the most effective way to reduce ambiguity for students and protect yourself when questions arise.

What should my syllabus say about AI?▼
Be explicit — vague language creates ambiguity that students will fill in their own way. Your policy should state: whether AI tools are permitted, in what contexts (brainstorming vs. drafting vs. editing), which tools if any are permitted, and how students should disclose AI use. The UNC Center for Faculty Excellence has syllabus language templates available.
A student submitted work I suspect was AI-generated. What do I do?▼
First, document your concerns and the specific evidence. AI detection tools are not reliable enough to use as sole evidence — do not cite a detection tool percentage as proof. Instead, have a conversation with the student about their process and ask them to explain their work. If you believe a violation occurred, refer to UNC's Honor Code process. Contact the Dean of Students Office for guidance before proceeding formally.

⚠ Do not rely on AI detection tools as sole evidence
Can I require students to use specific AI tools?▼
You can encourage or recommend specific tools, but requiring students to create accounts with third-party AI platforms raises FERPA considerations — student data may be collected by those platforms. If you want students to use an AI tool as part of coursework, consult with UNC ITS about which tools have appropriate data use agreements before making it a requirement.
How do I design assignments that are more resilient to AI misuse?▼
Focus on process over product: require drafts, reflections, or in-class components that demonstrate student thinking. Assignments that ask for personal experience, local or recent context, or discipline-specific analysis that would require genuine expertise to evaluate are harder to complete meaningfully with AI alone. The UNC Center for Faculty Excellence offers workshops on AI-resilient assignment design.

AI and Academic Integrity

UNC's Honor Code applies to AI use. Understanding it protects you — and helps you use AI in ways that genuinely support your education.

⚠️ Your professor's policy overrides everything else. Always check your syllabus first. If it does not address AI, ask your professor before using any AI tools for that course.
Is using AI on assignments cheating?▼
It depends entirely on your professor's policy and how you use it. Submitting AI-generated text as your own original work without disclosure is a violation of the Honor Code in most contexts. Using AI to brainstorm, get feedback on your draft, or check grammar — and disclosing that use — may be permitted. The key is transparency and following your specific course policy.

⚠ Always check your syllabus and ask your professor
What happens if I'm reported for AI misuse?▼
If a faculty member believes you violated the Honor Code, they will refer the matter to the Honor System. You will have the opportunity to respond. Outcomes range from no finding to grade penalties or suspension depending on severity and history. The most important thing: if you are ever unsure whether your AI use is permitted, ask your professor before submitting — not after.
Can professors tell if I used AI?▼
AI detection tools exist but are unreliable — they produce both false positives and false negatives. Professors cannot definitively prove AI use through detection tools alone. However, faculty know your writing. Significant shifts in style, vocabulary, or argument quality from previous work are often more telling than any detection tool. The safest approach is honest, disclosed use within your course policy.
How should I cite or disclose AI use?▼
When disclosure is required or appropriate, include a brief note — in a footnote, acknowledgments section, or author's note — that describes what tool you used and how. For example: "ChatGPT was used to generate an initial outline for this paper, which was then substantially revised by the author." Be specific about the nature of the use, not just that AI was involved.

Using AI Safely and Effectively

AI is most useful when it helps you think better, not when it thinks for you. Here is how to get genuine value from AI tools without compromising your integrity or your learning.

What are good ways to use AI that won't get me in trouble?▼
Generally safe uses include: brainstorming and idea generation before you write, asking AI to explain concepts you do not understand, getting feedback on a draft you have already written, checking grammar and clarity, and researching background context. The common thread: you are doing the thinking, and AI is supporting it. When AI does the primary intellectual work and you submit it as yours, that is where problems arise.
Should I enter personal or academic information into AI tools?▼
Be careful. Do not enter other students' information, confidential course materials, or sensitive personal data into consumer AI tools. Most consumer AI platforms store your inputs and may use them in training. For anything involving sensitive academic work, use UNC-approved tools or keep your prompts general and anonymized.

⚠ Never enter other people's information into AI tools
How do I know if AI output is accurate?▼
You often cannot tell just by reading it — AI generates confident-sounding text regardless of accuracy. Always verify facts, citations, and statistics from AI output against authoritative sources. AI tools frequently hallucinate — inventing citations, misrepresenting studies, or stating false information with complete confidence. Treat AI output as a starting point, not a source.
What is the best AI tool for students at UNC?▼
Microsoft Copilot is available through your UNC Microsoft 365 account and is the safest option for any work involving UNC systems or data. For general personal use, many students use ChatGPT or Claude — just be aware these are consumer tools with no UNC data agreement. Never use them for work involving other students' information or sensitive academic content.

✓ Use Copilot via your UNC M365 account for safest option

Everyday AI Use for UNC Staff

Most day-to-day AI questions come down to one thing: what kind of information are you working with? Use this guide to make confident decisions.

💡 The simplest rule: If you would not email this information to someone outside UNC, do not enter it into a consumer AI tool.
What tasks is AI actually useful for in my work?▼
AI tools are genuinely useful for drafting and editing emails or documents, summarizing long documents or meeting notes, brainstorming ideas and outlines, creating first drafts you then revise, formatting and organizing information, and generating talking points. These tasks do not require sensitive data and are well within the capabilities of approved tools like Microsoft Copilot.

✓ Great starting point for most staff
What should I never put into an AI tool?▼
Never enter: student records or grades, employee personnel information, health or medical data, social security numbers or financial account information, unpublished research data, information covered by a non-disclosure agreement, or any information marked confidential. If you are unsure whether information is sensitive, treat it as sensitive until you confirm otherwise with UNC ITS.

✗ Never enter restricted or personally identifiable information
My supervisor told me to use an AI tool I have not heard of. What should I do?▼
It is reasonable to ask whether the tool has been reviewed by UNC ITS before entering any work-related information. You can say: "I want to make sure we're using this in compliance with UNC's data policies — has ITS reviewed it?" That is not pushback, it is responsible practice. You can also check the UNC AI NOW tool comparison page or contact ITS directly to ask about a specific tool's status.
How do I get started with Microsoft Copilot?▼
Copilot is available through your existing UNC Microsoft 365 account — you may already have access. Open any Microsoft 365 app (Word, Outlook, Teams) and look for the Copilot icon. If you do not see it, contact UNC ITS to confirm your account has Copilot enabled. No separate sign-up is required if your account is licensed.

✓ Start here — it is your safest and most accessible option

Who to Contact When You Are Not Sure

Knowing when to escalate is a skill — and knowing who to contact makes it easy. Use this guide to get to the right person quickly.

My question is about data safety or tool approval▼
Contact: UNC ITS (Information Technology Services)

ITS handles tool security reviews, data classification questions, and institutional software agreements. They are the authoritative source on whether a specific AI tool has been reviewed for UNC use. Visit help.unc.edu or call the ITS Help Desk.

✓ ITS Help Desk: help.unc.edu
My question is about research data or IRB considerations▼
Contact: UNC Office of Research

For questions about AI use with research data, IRB protocols, grant compliance, or sponsor requirements, the Office of Research is your resource. They can connect you with a research compliance specialist for your specific situation.

✓ UNC Office of Research: research.unc.edu
My question is about student data or FERPA▼
Contact: University Registrar

FERPA questions related to student records and AI tool use should go to the University Registrar's office. They can advise on what constitutes protected student information and how it may or may not be used in AI contexts.

✓ University Registrar: registrar.unc.edu
I am not sure which office handles my question▼
Start with UNC AI NOW or ITS

If you are not sure where to begin, the UNC AI NOW help desk (coming soon) will route your question to the right office. In the meantime, ITS is the best first contact for most AI-related questions — they can refer you if needed. You will not be penalized for asking.

✓ When in doubt, start with ITS
